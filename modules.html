Hackathon

During the course of about a three week period, three classmates and I worked together on our hackathon, which at the time, was a pretty daunting task. Our proposal started with the idea to create a mental health resource, since our group noticed that there weren't a lot of resources available to students online about mental health, as a lot of the main ones that students would try to access at school were blocked by the OCDSB. To connect it to computer science though, we had to find a way to involve mental health in some sort of code or programming. So, we created a website using HTML, Javascript and CSS using Glitch as our web developer. This hackathon was the first time I created a website, so it helped me to get more comfortable with using HTML and CSS, while also learning what I consider a pretty basic skill in web development. Working through different files as a group was another thing I had to work on during this project, because I had never really worked on a larger project in a group before with computer science. Overall, this project helped me grow my skills regarding web development, especially in a fun group setting, and I even got to learn about more mental health resources that I will be able to use or provide to others in the future if they need them.

Learning To Learn - Cubing

Learning to learn, one of the first modules I completed at the beginning of this course, had my classmates and I dive into something I had never learned, experimented with, or even done in my life, cubing. By watching the documentary our class was provided, I got to learn about coding and the impacts that it had on many lives across the world, and the relationships and experiences that the people in the documentary were able to develop based on cubing as well. At the end of the documentary, my group was instructed to make a slideshow talking about a specific topic and how it was addressed or covered in the film, and my group chose the topic of friendship. I have been in many scenarios before in which I’ve had to try new things and make new connections, and cubing helped me do them all over again, just like the people in the documentary. I made some new friends through this module and through cubing, as I got two of my classmates to teach me how to solve a Pyraminx. Ever since then I’ve been improving my times, working to understand the best way to solve the Pyraminx and understand the algorithms that come with it too. Before I had started all of this, I had no idea how intense cubing could be, and how fun it would become for me. Learning and practicing the solving of a Pyraminx eventually led me to my best solve, at 17.17 seconds, a time I am proud of, speaking of my previous times from the beginning of my cubing journey, which started at around 3 minutes and 30 seconds. Not only did this module introduce me to something new through cubing, I got to understand how to solve a Pyraminx and what cubing meant to others, which helped me find new friends as well along the way.

Live Creative Coding Session

The live creative coding session module was probably my favoruite module to work on during the semester, as I was able to follow a coding tutorial by Dan Shiffman and recreate it with my groupmates and present our work to the class. We had chosen to re-create the snake game in P5, which proved to be a fun experience experimenting with creating a game in p5. Each of my groupmates, including myself, got the chance to create our own version of the game, and for this, I created a mini-game in which each time the snake eats a golden apple, their speed (also known as their score) increases. When they hit a wall or run into themselves, their speed and score resets, as your snake dies in game. Once we had finished our separate parts of the code, my group came together to create a slideshow and script for our project, in which I created the “script of code” to follow as we showed the class what we had learned from Dan Shiffman and what we had built upon it. Not only was this module a lot of fun, I also developed my skills working on code relating to a game and also my experiences working with others on the same base of code, which was particularly beneficial to me. I also got to complete two of my classmates’ projects as well, in which I followed them to create a clock and changed a picture using ascii related-code.

Game Jam/Phaser

Throughout the creation of this project, I developed my understanding of Phaser and JavaScript by using a Codecademy course as the base of my knowledge. The course was split into 4 sections, each teaching me something new about physics, animations, and Phaser game development in general. As me and my groupmates approached the end of the Codecademy course, we wanted to edit one of the games we had made earlier using the code provided to us, to create a "spin-off" of the game "Codey Jump." Experimenting with gravity, physics and bounce, we decided to change the properties of the platforms that Codey jumps on during the game to move, and then balance the rest of the game to make it actually function. Of course, with our time limit, we weren't able to complete the game, but we did make a prototype. Overall, this project allowed me to learn more about changing and developing a game, and was a lot of fun to work on as well. Changing physics and gravity, as well as the motions of platforms all play their role in our game, and I'll be able to use the skills I learned in this project to work towards another game like this one.
